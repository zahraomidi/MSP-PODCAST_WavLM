Learning from Annotation Uncertainty

Entropy-Aware Curriculum for Speech Emotion Recognition

Speech emotion recognition commonly relies on hard consensus labels that collapse annotator disagreement into a single category. This project studies distribution-based supervision in a WavLM-Base SER system on the 9-class MSP-Podcast 2.0 benchmark, comparing hard consensus training with targets derived from primary and merged primary-secondary annotator vote distributions.

Emotion Ambiguity and Annotation Uncertainty

Emotion perception is subjective and context dependent. Barrett et al. (2019) reconsidered the common view that emotion categories are reliably signaled by distinctive expressions, highlighting limited reliability, lack of specificity, and limited generalizability in how emotions are expressed and perceived.

This ambiguity is also present in speech emotion recognition. Multi-rater corpora such as MSP-Podcast 2.0 contain annotator disagreement, mixed emotional interpretations, and label variability across speakers and contexts. Collapsing these annotations into a single hard consensus label discards this information and obscures ambiguity patterns present in the data.

Our findings support treating annotation disagreement as structured perceptual information rather than label noise. Distribution-based supervision better preserves this ambiguity, improves alignment with annotator vote distributions, and reduces reliance on the residual Other category compared with hard consensus training.

Problem

Hard consensus labels collapse annotator disagreement into a single emotion category.

Supervision

We compare hard consensus labels with primary and merged primary-secondary annotator vote distributions.

Evaluation

We evaluate categorical emotion recognition, dimensional VAD prediction, and alignment with annotator vote distributions.

Conclusion

These findings suggest that SER systems should move beyond hard consensus labels toward distributional targets that better reflect listener disagreement.

Contributions

This study examines distribution-based supervision for speech emotion recognition by modeling annotator vote distributions rather than relying only on hard consensus labels.

Distributional supervision

We compare hard consensus training with targets derived from primary and merged primary-secondary annotator vote distributions.

Entropy-aware curriculum

We evaluate entropy filtering, entropy weighting, and reverse entropy curricula for ambiguity-aware training.

Multitask SER

We use a WavLM-Base system that jointly models categorical emotion and dimensional valence, arousal, and dominance.

Uncertainty analysis

We analyze distributional alignment, entropy-stratified performance, and the role of the residual Other category.

Supervision Targets

We compare hard consensus labels with distributional targets derived from annotator vote distributions. Primary and merged targets provide alternative forms of categorical supervision while preserving different amounts of annotation uncertainty.

Hard

Consensus emotion label used as a one-hot categorical target.

Primary

Distribution over emotion categories derived from primary annotator vote counts.

M90

Merged primary-secondary distribution using a 0.9P/0.1S target setting.

M80

Merged primary-secondary distribution using a 0.8P/0.2S target setting.

Supervision and Annotation Uncertainty

Per-class annotation entropy. Entropy is shown under primary and merged primary-secondary vote distributions. Merged targets use 0.9P/0.1S and 0.8P/0.2S settings.

Model Architecture

Model architecture. WavLM-Base is used as the backbone, followed by a TC-GRU model. The model jointly predicts a discrete emotion distribution and dimensional emotion attributes including valence, activation, and dominance.

Main Findings

Distributional supervision changes what the model learns from ambiguous annotations. We evaluate both categorical performance and alignment with annotator vote distributions.

Distributional alignment

Distributional objectives consistently improve alignment with annotator vote distributions, yielding lower JSD and KLD.

Hard supervision

Gains under hard supervision are partly driven by assigning ambiguous samples to the residual Other category.

Entropy-stratified evaluation

High-ambiguity utterances remain challenging under both hard and distribution-based supervision.

Perceptual uncertainty

Distribution-based supervision provides more faithful modeling of perceptual uncertainty than hard consensus labels.

Confusion Matrices Across Supervision Settings

Confusion matrices are shown for the supervision targets and training settings reported in the paper. Each thumbnail can be opened in a new tab for full-resolution inspection.

Confusion-matrix analysis

The confusion matrices show how supervision choice affects class-wise prediction behavior. Hard-label systems concentrate many ambiguous samples into dominant classes and the residual Other category, while distribution-based objectives redistribute uncertainty across structured emotion categories. Entropy-aware curricula further change these prediction patterns, although low-resource and high-ambiguity classes such as Fear, Disgust, Contempt, and Surprise remain challenging. This supports the paper's finding that distributional supervision provides a more faithful model of annotator disagreement than hard consensus labels.
